Static Results Tab

Located at static_analyzer/tabs/static_results_tab.py, the Results tab (implemented as the mixin class StaticResultsTab) is the terminal reporting stage of the Static Analyzer workspace. Whereas the Fracture Map, Histogram, CDF, Rose Plot, Stereonet, Connectivity, and Intensity & Density tabs each perform a specialised geometric or statistical analysis on a loaded fracture network, the Results tab aggregates the outputs of every one of those analyses into a single, coherent scientific record. It serves three complementary purposes: consolidation — collecting geometry, length, orientation, connectivity/topology, intensity/density, and probability-distribution-fitting results into one unified dashboard; verification — exposing every derived quantity side by side so an analyst can sanity-check the network before it is carried into downstream engineering work; and reporting and dissemination — exporting the same figures into portable PDF, Word, plain-text, Excel, and CSV formats suitable for a report or publication, without requiring the recipient to have FracSTAT installed.

The Results tab performs no geometric or statistical computation of its own; it is a read-only presentation-and-export layer. Every numeric value shown originates from calculations already performed by sibling tabs — principally the Fracture Map tab for geometry, the Histogram tab for length/mode statistics, the Connectivity tab for I-Y-X topology, the Intensity & Density tab for P21/P20 circular-scanline intensity, and the CDF tab for maximum-likelihood distribution fitting. This design keeps the expensive statistical machinery implemented in exactly one place, while the Results tab is responsible only for formatting, tabulating, and exporting whatever those other tabs have already produced.

1. Purpose and Scope

The Results tab is where geometry, length, orientation, connectivity, intensity/density, and distribution-fitting results computed elsewhere in the Static Analyzer are brought together into one screen. It exists so a user never has to visit every individual tab to assemble a full characterisation of a fracture network, and so that the finished characterisation can be exported for use outside FracSTAT entirely.

  1. Consolidation — collects every derived quantity computed across the workspace into one unified dashboard.
  2. Verification — lets an analyst sanity-check node counts, connectivity indices, distribution parameters, and goodness-of-fit statistics side by side before the results are used in downstream geomechanical work (e.g. slip-tendency analysis in the Dynamic Analyzer).
  3. Reporting and dissemination — exports the same figures used inside FracSTAT into portable, shareable, and archivable document formats (PDF, Word, plain text, Excel, CSV) suitable for a technical report, thesis, or publication.

2. Interface Overview

The Results tab occupies the ninth (rightmost) position in the Static Analyzer's main CTkTabview, alongside Fracture Map, Histogram, CDF, Rose Plot, Stereonet Plot, Connectivity, and Intensity & Density. Its layout follows the same two-column convention used throughout the Static Analyzer: a narrow, scrollable left-hand sidebar containing action controls, and a wide right-hand content panel containing the report itself.

2.1 Sidebar (Left Panel)

The sidebar is a vertically scrollable CTkScrollableFrame containing three stacked cards:

4.2 Inner Tab Controls

The Summary and Raw Segment Data pages are selected via a standard CTkTabview segmented control at the top of the content panel. No additional filtering, sorting, or display controls are provided on the Summary page; every statistic card is always shown in full. The Raw Segment Data page's ttk.Treeview supports native column-header click-to-sort behaviour and independent horizontal/vertical scrollbars, but exposes no FracSTAT-specific filtering controls.

4.3 Lazy-Loading Behaviour

The Results tab is registered with a wrapped CTkTabview command handler so that the (potentially expensive) work of populating the dashboard and raw-data table is deferred until the user actually clicks the Results tab, rather than being recomputed on every geometry change elsewhere in the workspace. A boolean flag, self._results_stale, is set whenever refresh_static_results() is called from elsewhere in the application (for example after loading a new file or changing the profile scale); the flag is only cleared once the tab is actually visited and _load_results_with_loader() has completed.

5. Mathematical Background

This section defines, with full mathematical notation, every quantity computed or re-derived inside the Results module. Quantities that are computed upstream (in the Histogram, Connectivity, Intensity & Density, or CDF tabs) and simply displayed here are also defined, since the Results tab is where a user most commonly encounters them assembled together for the first time.

5.1 Geometry and Length Statistics

Let the fracture network consist of ns segments grouped into nt traces. For each segment i, let Li denote its length in the project's display unit (m, cm, mm, or km, selected on the Fracture Map tab).

Total Length = Σ Lᵢ (i = 1 … nₛ) Mean Segment Length = (1/nₛ) · Σ Lᵢ

Trace length is the sum of the constituent segment lengths for each trace; per-trace totals are stored in the TraceLengthM column of the results table (or computed on demand as a group-wise sum).

Trace Lengthⱼ = Σ Lᵢ for all segments i belonging to trace j

For both the segment-length and trace-length populations, the Results tab reports the maximum, minimum, median, sample standard deviation (using Bessel's correction, ddof = 1), and the statistical mode. Because fracture lengths are continuous, the mode is estimated from a histogram (Section 6.1) rather than from an exact value count.

5.2 Circular (Axial) Orientation Statistics

Fracture strikes are axial data — a fracture measured at azimuth θ is physically indistinguishable from one measured at θ + 180°. Ordinary (linear) circular statistics would therefore be biased; the module instead uses the doubled-angle method of Mardia and Jupp for axial data. Each strike θᵢ (already folded into 0°–180°) is doubled to 2θᵢ, mapped onto the full circle, and averaged as a set of unit vectors:

C̄ = (1/n) Σ cos(2θᵢ) S̄ = (1/n) Σ sin(2θᵢ) R = √(C̄² + S̄²) Circular Mean = ½ · atan2(S̄, C̄) (mod 180°) Circular Std. Dev. = ½ · √(−2 ln R) (in degrees, after conversion from radians)

R (the mean resultant length, 0 ≤ R ≤ 1) measures the concentration of the strike population: R close to 1 indicates a strongly preferred orientation (a single dominant fracture set), while R close to 0 indicates near-uniform, poorly organised orientations. The Dominant Orientation statistic reported alongside the circular mean is instead the histogram-modal bin centre (Section 6.1) — it can differ from the circular mean whenever the strike distribution is multi-modal (e.g. two conjugate fracture sets), since the circular mean of two symmetric peaks straddling a gap is not itself a peak.

5.3 Connectivity and Topology (I-Y-X Node Theory)

The connectivity statistics implement the standard structural-geology I-Y-X node classification (Sanderson & Nixon, 2015 and related literature). Every fracture-trace endpoint or intersection is classified as one of three node types:

Node TypeSymbolGeological Meaning
IsolatedIA free-standing fracture tip that terminates without abutting or crossing another fracture; contributes nothing to fluid connectivity at that point.
AbuttingYOne fracture terminates against the side of another (a T- or Y-shaped junction); a single-sided hydraulic connection.
CrossingXTwo fractures cross one another, each continuing beyond the intersection; a fully connected, four-way hydraulic junction.

Let N_I, N_Y, N_X denote the counts of each node type. Two derived connectivity indices are computed:

Connections per Line: N_L = (N_I + N_Y) / 2 C_L = 2(N_X + N_Y) / N_L Connections per Branch: N_B = (N_I + 3N_Y + 4N_X) / 2 C_B = (3N_Y + 4N_X) / N_B

C_L and C_B are Sanderson-style average-connections-per-line and average-connections-per-branch indices; both increase toward their theoretical maxima (4 for C_L on a fully crossing network, 2 for C_B) as the network becomes progressively better connected. The Connected Fraction reported in the summary is (N_Y + N_X) / (N_I + N_Y + N_X), i.e. the proportion of nodes that are not simply dead-end tips.

A branch is the segment of trace lying between two adjacent nodes (of any type). Every branch is further classified by the type of node at each of its two ends into one of three topological classes: I-I (isolated at both ends), I-C (isolated at one end, connected — Y or X — at the other), and C-C (connected at both ends). Their counts, nII, nIC, nCC, are reported directly, and feed the branch-topology (Sanderson) ternary diagram on the Connectivity tab. The Dimensionless Intensity, B22, follows the standard normalisation of fracture intensity by the characteristic sampling-area length scale:

B22 = (Total Length)² / Sample Area

5.4 Fracture Intensity and Density (P21, P20)

The Intensity & Density tab implements the Mauldon circular-scanline method for unbiased areal estimation of one- and zero-dimensional intensity measures, and the Results tab reproduces its two headline outputs verbatim:

P21 (areal fracture intensity) = Total trace length within sampled circles / (4 × r) [units: length⁻¹] P20 (areal fracture density) = Number of trace endpoints within sampled circles / (2π × r²) [units: length⁻²]

where r is the scan-circle radius. P21 and P20 are classic Mauldon (2001) circular-window intensity/density estimators; they are unbiased with respect to fracture orientation, unlike simple scanline counts, because a circular sampling window intersects fractures of every strike with equal probability.

5.5 Probability Distribution Fitting (CDF Backfill)

The Results tab reproduces the parameters, information criteria, and goodness-of-fit statistics of every maximum-likelihood distribution model fitted on the CDF tab: Power-law, Exponential, Log-normal, Weibull (implemented as a stretched exponential), and Truncated Power-law. The complete mathematical derivation of these fits (log-likelihood construction, AIC/AICc/BIC, Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistics, bootstrap confidence intervals, and the parametric bootstrap goodness-of-fit p-value) is the responsibility of the CDF tab and is documented there; the Results tab's role is purely to surface the cached fit object's parameters into the dashboard. For completeness, the defining forms of each survival function P(L>x) for x ≥ x_min are:

Power-law: P(L>x) = (x / x_min)^(−α+1) Exponential: P(L>x) = exp(−λx) Log-normal: P(L>x) = ½·erfc[(ln x − μ) / (σ√2)] Weibull (stretched exp.): P(L>x) = exp[−(λx)^β] Truncated Power-law: P(L>x) = C · x^(−α+1) · exp(−λx)

6. Algorithms

6.1 Histogram-Based Mode Estimation

Because fracture segment/trace lengths and strike angles are continuous quantities, the module estimates the statistical mode by binning the data with numpy.histogram using the same bin count as the Histogram tab's own control (falling back to 20 bins if that control has not yet been built), then returning the centre of whichever bin holds the highest count. If the data range degenerates to a single value (max equals min), that value is returned directly. This histogram-derived mode is reused for three quantities: Segment Mode Length, Trace Mode Length, and the Dominant Orientation (Strike) bin centre.

python 
def _histogram_mode(values_arr: np.ndarray, n_bins: int) -> float:
    if values_arr.size == 0:
        return 0.0
    v_min, v_max = float(values_arr.min()), float(values_arr.max())
    if v_min == v_max:
        return v_min
    step = (v_max - v_min) / n_bins
    centres = np.arange(v_min, v_max + step / 2, step)
    edges = np.concatenate([[centres[0] - step / 2], centres + step / 2])
    counts, _ = np.histogram(values_arr, bins=edges)
    if counts.size == 0:
        return v_min
    return float(centres[int(np.argmax(counts))])

The _histogram_mode() helper, shared by the Segment Mode Length, Trace Mode Length, and Dominant Orientation statistics.

6.2 Dynamic Column-Set Resolution for Raw Data Export

Because FracSTAT accepts several structurally different input formats (SVG trace geometry, spatial Excel/CSV tables, legacy orientation-only fracture-plane tables, and Global CMT-style focal-mechanism tables), the Results tab cannot assume a fixed set of columns exists in self.results. The _raw_data_column_set() algorithm inspects the loaded segment objects to determine which of three column templates applies:

Detected FormatDetection RuleDisplayed / Exported Columns
Focal mechanismLat column present and at least one non-null valueID, Trace, PlaneName, EventLabel, Region, Lat, Lon, StrikeDeg, DipDeg, Rake
Orientation-onlysegments[0]._has_map_geometry is FalseID, Trace, Segment, StrikeDeg, DipDeg, Length_m, Aperture
Spatial (SVG or coordinate-bearing Excel)default / has_geometry TrueID, Trace, Segment, X1, Y1, X2, Y2, Length_m, Aperture, StrikeDeg, TraceLengthM
python 
def _raw_data_column_set(self, df):
    segs = getattr(self, "segments", None)
    has_geometry = getattr(segs[0], "_has_map_geometry", True) if segs else True
    has_latlon = "Lat" in df.columns and df["Lat"].notna().any()

    if has_latlon:
        candidates = ["ID", "Trace", "PlaneName", "EventLabel", "Region",
                      "Lat", "Lon", "StrikeDeg", "DipDeg", "Rake"]
    elif not has_geometry:
        candidates = ["ID", "Trace", "Segment", "StrikeDeg", "DipDeg",
                      "Length_m", "Aperture"]
    else:
        candidates = ["ID", "Trace", "Segment", "X1", "Y1", "X2", "Y2",
                      "Length_m", "Aperture", "StrikeDeg", "TraceLengthM"]

    display_cols = [c for c in candidates if c in df.columns]
    if not display_cols:
        display_cols = [c for c in df.columns if c != "Color"]
    return display_cols

Only columns that are actually present in the DataFrame are retained; if none of the candidate columns exist (an unexpected edge case) the routine falls back to displaying every column except the internal Color field. The same three-way branching, together with a DipDeg-drop rule (removing the DipDeg column entirely when every value is the default 90° placeholder) and a geometry-column drop for orientation-only data, is applied a second time inside _get_export_df() before Excel/CSV/Text export, so the exported spreadsheet never contains meaningless placeholder geometry.

6.3 Unit-Aware Column Renaming

Before both on-screen display and file export, length-bearing columns are renamed to embed the currently selected display unit, e.g. Length_m becomes Length_cm when the user has selected centimetres on the Fracture Map tab's unit selector, and TraceLengthM becomes TraceLength_km for kilometres. This prevents ambiguity when a user changes units mid-session or shares an exported file with a collaborator.

6.4 CDF Model Backfill from the Fit Cache

Rather than re-running the (potentially slow) maximum-likelihood fitting procedure, _collect_cdf_summary_values() reads the most recently computed entry from the CDF tab's internal fit cache (self._cdf_fit_cache) — a dictionary keyed by a fingerprint of the input data and the active fitting settings — and extracts the powerlaw.Fit object's five sub-model attributes (power_law, exponential, lognormal, stretched_exponential, truncated_power_law) directly. From that single cached fit, the algorithm reconstructs, for every one of the five distributions simultaneously, its fitted parameter(s), 95% (or user-selected level) bootstrap confidence interval (if a bootstrap run has been performed), tail sample size and percentage, log-likelihood, AIC, BIC, the powerlaw library's native Kolmogorov–Smirnov D statistic, an independently computed SciPy asymptotic KS statistic and p-value, and — if available — the parametric-bootstrap goodness-of-fit p-value. This backfill approach means the Results tab always shows every candidate distribution's statistics side by side, even though the CDF tab's own plot only ever actively displays the single model currently selected in its dropdown.

6.5 Textual current_stats Parsing

A second pass over the CDF tab's self.current_stats dictionary (an ordered, flat dictionary populated live as the CDF tab redraws) recovers information that is not part of the cached Fit object itself: the raw descriptive statistics block, any manually overridden Truncated Power-law parameters (α, λ fixed by the user rather than found by MLE), bootstrap confidence-interval strings, and — when present — the full Model Comparison / Auto Best Fit narrative text and pairwise likelihood-ratio table. Section markers in the dictionary (keys formatted as "── SectionName ──") are used to prefix subsequent keys with their originating distribution name (e.g. turning a bare "xmin" key into "Power-law xmin"), except for the Descriptive, Auto Best Fit, and Model Comparison sections, whose keys are already self-describing and are left unprefixed.

6.6 Dynamic Comparison-Table Rendering

When a CDF Model Comparison or Auto Best Fit run has been performed, its result is a pipe-delimited (|) plain-text table embedded as a single multi-line string value under the key "Comparison Table". The Results tab's _render_comparison_table() parses this string line by line: lines containing at least one pipe character are split into cells and rendered as a horizontally scrollable grid (alternating row shading, bold header row); lines without a pipe character (dashed separators are skipped, footnote/interpretation lines are kept) are rendered as italicised note paragraphs beneath the grid. The identical parsing logic is duplicated, with format-appropriate output primitives, inside the PDF export (ReportLab Table/Paragraph objects) and the Word export (python-docx table rows) so that the printed report matches the on-screen dashboard exactly.

7. Internal Workflow

The Results tab participates in the application-wide tab-change and dirty-tracking machinery already used by the other Static Analyzer tabs, extended with its own staleness flag so that its two moderately expensive population routines — _populate_summary() and _populate_raw_data() — are only ever run when actually needed.

7.1 Construction Sequence

  1. _build_static_results_tab() is called once, during workspace start-up, after every other tab has been built. It constructs the sidebar cards, the inner Summary/Raw Segment Data tabview, and every statistic-card label placeholder (each pre-populated with the em-dash "—" as a not-yet-computed sentinel).
  2. It then wraps the outer CTkTabview's existing on-tab-change command (which already performs autosave and other bookkeeping for the other tabs) with an additional check: if the newly selected tab is "Results" and self._results_stale is True, _load_results_with_loader() is invoked.
  3. self._results_stale is initialised to True, so the very first visit to the Results tab always performs a full population, regardless of whether any data has been loaded yet (in which case the population routines simply render their respective empty states).
python 
self._results_stale = True

existing_cmd = self.tabs.cget("command")
def _on_tab_click():
    if existing_cmd:
        existing_cmd()
    if self.tabs.get() == "Results" and getattr(self, "_results_stale", False):
        self._load_results_with_loader()

self.tabs.configure(command=_on_tab_click)

The wrapped tab-change command installed at the end of _build_static_results_tab(), chaining onto whatever command the CTkTabview already had.

7.2 Staleness Propagation

refresh_static_results(force=False) is the single external entry point other parts of the workspace call whenever underlying data changes (e.g. at the end of the multi-step refresh_all_tabs() chain in static_app.py, itself triggered after loading a new SVG/Excel file, changing the profile scale, or editing the Y-axis azimuth). It always sets self._results_stale = True. If force=True (used by the explicit Refresh button) or the Results tab already happens to be the active tab, it immediately calls _load_results_with_loader(); otherwise it only updates a small status label ("Data changed. Click tab to load.") and clears the raw-data table, deferring the actual work until the user next clicks the tab.

7.3 Loader Routine

_load_results_with_loader() shows the shared global loading overlay ("Compiling Results Data…"), then — after a short after() delay so the overlay has a chance to paint before the main thread is blocked — calls _populate_summary() followed by _populate_raw_data(), clears the stale flag, and hides the overlay. Both population routines run synchronously on the Tkinter main thread; no background threading is used for the Results tab itself (unlike the SVG/Excel loaders in the Fracture Map tab, which do use worker threads for the geometry recomputation that feeds this tab).

7.4 Summary Population

_populate_summary() calls _collect_all_summary_values() to build a single flat dictionary of every statistic (merging the geometry/connectivity/intensity computations described in Section 6 with the CDF backfill dictionary from _collect_cdf_summary_values()), then walks every pre-built label reference in self._summary_section_refs, looking up and writing the corresponding formatted string (or the em-dash placeholder if the key is absent for the current dataset). Finally, it clears and, if applicable, rebuilds the dynamic Model Comparison table beneath the main grid of cards.

7.5 Raw Data Population

_populate_raw_data() first tears down any previously built ttk.Treeview (since the column set can legitimately change between loads — e.g. switching from an SVG file to a focal-mechanism Excel table), determines the appropriate column set via _raw_data_column_set() (Section 6.2), configures ttk.Style Treeview colours to track the current CustomTkinter light/dark appearance, and inserts one row per DataFrame record with values pre-formatted by _raw_fmt_value() (four-significant-figure formatting for floats, an em-dash for missing values, and unmodified strings otherwise).

python 
def _raw_fmt_value(val):
    if isinstance(val, float):
        if pd.isna(val):
            return "—"
        return f"{val:.4g}"
    if val is None or (isinstance(val, str) and not val.strip()):
        return "—"
    return str(val)

The value-formatting helper used for every cell inserted into the Raw Segment Data Treeview.

8. Statistical Methods

The Results tab presents statistics from four broad families: descriptive geometry statistics, circular orientation statistics, discrete topological (graph-theoretic) connectivity statistics, and parametric probability-distribution fitting statistics for the tail of the fracture-length distribution. The table below inventories every statistical quantity shown on the Summary dashboard, its source, and its interpretation.

CardFieldStatistical Meaning
Geometry OverviewTraces / Segments / Total NodesSample sizes: number of distinct fracture traces, constituent straight segments, and I+Y+X topological nodes.

Connectivity (I-Y-X)Connected Fraction / C_L / C_B / B22 / Branch classesTopological connectivity indices from I-Y-X node theory (Section 5.3).

9. Visualization

9.1 Summary Dashboard Layout

The Summary page uses a three-column, uniform-width CTkScrollableFrame grid. Each statistic family is rendered as an independently bordered, rounded-corner "card" containing a bold section header, a thin divider rule, and a two-column key/value grid with alternating subtle row shading for readability. Card placement follows a fixed (row, column, column-span) layout: the Geometry Overview, Length Statistics, and Connectivity cards occupy the first row (one card per column); Angle/Strike Statistics, Fracture Intensity & Density, and Data Source occupy the second row; the CDF Descriptive Statistics card spans the full three-column width in the third row (reflecting its larger field count); and the five per-distribution CDF cards plus the Model Comparison/Best Fit card occupy the fourth and fifth rows.

Every key/value row is bound to a <Configure> event that recomputes wraplength for both the field-name and value labels proportionally to the row's live pixel width, so that long field names (e.g. "Truncated Power-law GOF p-value (bootstrap, refit KS)") remain fully readable at any window size without being clipped or overlapping the adjacent value column.

9.2 Comparison Table Widget

When rendered, the dynamic Model Comparison table (Section 6.6) appears beneath the main card grid inside its own bordered card, using a horizontally scrollable CTkScrollableFrame so that wide multi-column comparison tables (Distribution, k, Log-Likelihood, KS, KS p, AIC, ΔAIC, AICc, BIC, ΔBIC, Akaike weight, BIC weight, LRT R, LRT p, Rank, Recommendation — sixteen columns) remain fully navigable without shrinking individual cell text below legibility.

9.3 Raw Segment Data Table

The Raw Segment Data page renders a native ttk.Treeview configured with a 65-pixel row height (tall enough to display two-line wrapped text where needed), synchronized vertical and horizontal ttk.Scrollbar widgets, and Treeview/Treeview.Heading ttk.Style rules that adapt background, foreground, and selection-highlight colours to the current CustomTkinter appearance mode. Column widths are set individually per field (130 px for text-heavy fields such as EventLabel, PlaneName, and Region; 90 px for numeric fields), and column headings display the unit-qualified name for length fields.

10. Advanced Features

10.1 Multi-Format Report Generation

The three summary-export routines (_export_pdf, _export_word, _export_txt) all draw from the identical section/field ordering list, guaranteeing that a PDF, Word, and plain-text export of the same analysis session are structurally identical in content and section order, differing only in their native formatting idioms (ReportLab flowables and TableStyle for PDF; python-docx Heading/Table objects with RGBColor-styled runs for Word; fixed-width padded strings for text). This consistency is a deliberate design choice so that a laboratory or consultancy can standardise on any one of the three formats without losing information relative to the others.

10.2 Condensed Comparison-Table Typography for PDF

Because the Model Comparison table can carry up to sixteen columns, the PDF exporter uses a markedly smaller (5.5 pt) Paragraph-wrapped font for its cells (versus the 9–10 pt body font used elsewhere in the same document) and lets ReportLab auto-size column widths, so that the full comparison table fits within an A4 page without truncation. The Word exporter applies an equivalent reduction (6.5 pt) together with centre-aligned cell paragraphs for the same reason.

10.3 Unicode Superscript Normalisation

Several statistics (notably the P21/P20 intensity and density units, e.g. "m⁻¹" and "m⁻²") are rendered with true Unicode superscript characters for on-screen display, since CustomTkinter labels support arbitrary Unicode glyphs. Both the PDF and text/Word exporters proactively replace these Unicode superscripts with ASCII-safe equivalents ("^-1", "^-2", "^2") before writing them into the exported document, avoiding missing-glyph rendering issues in some PDF viewers or older Word installations.

10.4 Fingerprint-Based Change Detection

To avoid unnecessary recomputation, several upstream tabs (most notably the CDF tab, whose MLE fitting is the single most expensive computation in the whole workspace) use a lightweight fingerprint — typically (row count, rounded sum of a key numeric column, active setting values) — to decide whether a data-dependent cache entry is still valid. The Results tab's backfill logic in Section 6.4 relies on, and benefits from, this same caching discipline: it never triggers a new distribution fit itself, so opening the Results tab is always fast, but it always reflects whatever the most recent CDF computation produced.

12. Practical Examples

12.1 Example 1 — Verifying a Newly Loaded SVG Trace Map

  1. Load an SVG fracture trace map via the Fracture Map tab's Load SVG button, and confirm the map renders as expected.
  2. Open the Connectivity tab once to trigger its (independently lazy) I-Y-X computation, since the Results tab's Connectivity card depends on that cache being populated.
  3. Click the Results tab. Confirm the Geometry Overview card's Traces and Segments counts match the number of distinct traces visible on the map, and that Total Nodes (I+Y+X) is a sensible fraction of twice the segment count (each segment contributes two endpoints, some of which coincide at shared nodes).
  4. Inspect the Length Statistics card: an unexpectedly large Segment Max Length relative to the Median often indicates a digitising error (e.g. two traces that should have been separate objects were accidentally merged into one long path during SVG creation).
  5. Export the summary as a PDF for inclusion in a field-mapping QC report using Export to PDF (.pdf).

12.2 Example 2 — Selecting a Length Distribution Model for Reporting

  1. On the CDF tab, set Distribution Fit Model to Auto Best Fit and, optionally, enable Bootstrap CI & GOF with a chosen resample count.
  2. Switch to the Results tab. The CDF: Power-law, CDF: Exponential, CDF: Log-normal, CDF: Weibull, and CDF: Truncated Power-law cards will all be populated simultaneously from the single cached fit, letting every candidate model's AIC, BIC, and KS statistic be compared at a glance without switching the CDF tab's dropdown five times.
  3. Read the CDF: Model Comparison / Best Fit card's Final Recommendation and Selection Warning fields to determine whether the automatic procedure found a statistically unambiguous winner or whether several models remain practically indistinguishable (in which case the recommended approach is to report the model-averaged parameter estimates shown under Model-Averaging Support rather than a single "best" model).
  4. Scroll to the dynamically rendered Comparison Table beneath the card grid for the full pairwise likelihood-ratio evidence underlying that recommendation, then export the whole page to Word for inclusion in a manuscript's supplementary materials.

12.3 Example 3 — Exporting Raw Data for External GIS or Statistical Software

  1. Switch to the Raw Segment Data inner tab and confirm the displayed column set matches the loaded data format (spatial X1/Y1/X2/Y2 columns for SVG data; Lat/Lon/PlaneName for a focal-mechanism table).
  2. Use Export to CSV (.csv) in the sidebar to produce a file directly importable into GIS software (e.g. QGIS) for spatial cross-referencing against other datasets, or Export to Excel (.xlsx) for further manual analysis in a spreadsheet.

13. Geological Interpretation

The Results dashboard is designed to answer, in one screen, the questions a structural geologist or reservoir engineer typically asks of a digitised fracture network.

13.1 Is the network well connected?

High values of Connected Fraction and C_L/C_B, together with a large proportion of X and C-C nodes relative to I nodes, indicate a network capable of supporting significant fluid or gas through-flow — relevant to reservoir permeability, contaminant transport, and slope-stability seepage analysis. A network dominated by I nodes and I-I branches, by contrast, behaves closer to a set of isolated, disconnected discontinuities.

13.2 Is the network scale-invariant (fractal) or does it have a characteristic length?

A Power-law fit with a low AIC/BIC relative to the Exponential and Log-normal alternatives, combined with a high KS p-value, supports treating the fracture-length population as approximately scale-invariant over the sampled range — a common finding for tectonic joint sets and consistent with self-similar brittle deformation processes. An Exponential or Log-normal preference instead suggests a characteristic length scale exists, often associated with mechanical layer thickness control on fracture propagation (bed-confined jointing) or a specific genetic mechanism with a finite driving-stress range.

13.3 Does the network show a preferred orientation, and how strong is it?

A low Circular Std. Dev. paired with a Dominant Orientation close to the Circular Mean indicates a single, tectonically controlled fracture set (e.g. joints formed under a consistent regional stress field). A high circular standard deviation, or a Dominant Orientation that diverges substantially from the circular mean, is a signature of either two conjugate sets or a poorly organised, possibly non-tectonic (e.g. cooling-joint or weathering-related) fracture population — best confirmed by cross-checking against the Rose Plot tab.

13.4 How intense is fracturing, and is it representative of the true rock mass?

P21 and P20 provide areal-density measures directly comparable across different outcrops, scanlines, or digital elevation models, because the Mauldon circular-scanline method removes the orientation bias inherent in straight-line scanline sampling (the classical Terzaghi correction problem). Comparing P21 values between multiple mapped domains within the same project is a standard way to identify structural domains or damage zones associated with faults.

14. Implementation Notes

  • The module is implemented as a Python mixin class (StaticResultsTab) intended to be combined via multiple inheritance with StaticAnalyzerApp and its sibling tab mixins; it therefore freely reads and writes attributes (self.results, self.segments, self._fluid_connectivity, self.current_stats, and others) that are defined by those sibling classes rather than by itself.
  • All numeric formatting in the Summary dashboard uses Python's general (%.4g) or fixed-precision (%.4f, %.3f) format specifiers chosen per-field to balance readability against precision; users requiring full machine precision should use the raw-data or CDF-tab-native exports rather than the formatted Summary text.
  • PDF export depends on the reportlab package; Word export depends on python-docx; both are optional dependencies and their absence is caught and reported to the user via a friendly "Missing library" message box rather than a raw stack trace.
  • Excel export depends on openpyxl, again with a graceful ImportError message.
  • The export file-save dialog defaults its initial directory to the application-wide configured export path (Settings page → Export → Default export directory) when one has been configured and still exists on disk, falling back to the operating system's default save location otherwise.
  • Because the CDF backfill (_collect_cdf_summary_values) reads only the most recently cached fit-cache entry, switching the CDF tab's data source (e.g. from Segment Lengths to Trace Total Lengths) or its fitting cutoff (x_min mode) and then immediately visiting the Results tab without letting the CDF tab redraw first can momentarily show statistics computed under the previous settings; visiting the CDF tab at least once after any such change ensures the cache — and therefore the Results tab — reflects the new settings.

15. Limitations

LimitationDescription
No independent CDF computationThe Results tab performs no independent statistical computation for the CDF distribution-fitting section; it is entirely dependent on the CDF tab having been visited (so that its fit cache is populated) at least once during the current session. If no CDF fit has ever been run, the five per-distribution cards remain blank (em-dash placeholders).
No row cap on raw dataThe raw-data table and all raw-data exports are not row-capped, unlike some other FracSTAT tables; extremely large fracture networks (tens of thousands of segments) may take a perceptible amount of time to populate and scroll smoothly.
Mode estimates are bin-count sensitiveHistogram-derived mode estimates (Section 6.1) are sensitive to the chosen bin count; because that bin count is shared with the Histogram tab's own control, changing it there will change the reported Mode values here on the next refresh, which is expected behaviour but can surprise users unaware of the shared setting.
Single active datasetThe module assumes a single, currently active fracture-network dataset; it has no facility for comparing or exporting statistics across multiple loaded projects simultaneously.
No intensity/density for geographic dataGeographic (Lat/Lon, focal-mechanism) datasets do not populate the Map Area, Scan Circle Radius, or P21/P20 fields, since circular-scanline intensity/density estimation is only meaningful for planar, metrically-scaled trace geometry.
